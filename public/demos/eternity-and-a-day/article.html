Essay — March 15, 2026

The Words We Buy From Strangers

By Elena Papadimitriou

Language & Memory

In the winter markets of Thessaloniki, between stalls selling dried figs and counterfeit watches, there is a boy who sells words. Not written words — spoken ones. He approaches you with a small notebook and offers a word in Albanian, or Arabic, or a language he says has no name, and he tells you what it means, and if you want to keep it, you pay him what you think it’s worth.

Most people walk past. Some laugh. A few stop, not because they need a word, but because they understand what the boy is really selling: the idea that language is not free. That every word we use was once someone’s invention, someone’s gift, someone’s act of desperation against silence.

I bought three words from him last February. The first meant something like ‘the light that comes through fog.’ The second meant ‘a journey that changes direction without the traveler noticing.’ The third he said was untranslatable — it described the feeling of standing at a border and belonging to both sides and neither.

I have been thinking about those words ever since. Not their meanings — I have probably misremembered those — but the transaction itself. The idea that a word could be worth something specific. That you could hold it in your hand like a coin and decide: is this worth keeping?

We treat language as if it were air — abundant, shared, free. But the boy in the market knows better. He knows that the rarest words are the ones that exist in only one language, the ones that die when the last speaker dies, the ones that describe feelings so specific they have no passport.

Linguists call these words ‘lacunae’ — gaps in a language where a concept exists in the world but not in the vocabulary. Every language has them. English has no single word for the Portuguese ‘saudade,’ that bittersweet longing for something absent. Greek has no equivalent of the Japanese ‘mono no aware,’ the gentle sadness of passing things.

But the boy’s words were different. They weren’t from any language I could identify. They might have been invented. They might have been fragments of something older, carried across borders in the pockets of refugees, worn smooth by use, polished by the friction of displacement.

Does it matter? A word’s origin is less important than its arrival. What matters is that someone needed to say something that had no name, and so they made one. This is the oldest act of creation: naming the unnamed. Finding syllables for the fog.
